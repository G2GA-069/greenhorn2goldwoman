CHAPTER 1 OF 11 — WELCOME
Canary Wharf, London — 17th Floor, SBCI (Subtrax Model Review)

23:47. One AM tomorrow. Fluorescent light hums above a desk the size of a small car. Trace Flint, 23, analyst second-year, sits alone. The floor is hollow—empty seats, darkened screens, the faint whir of servers below. Nobody else has been here since 19:30. She was supposed to leave at 18:00. She ate a sad desk salad around 19:15 and stayed.

Her screen glows white: Subtrax_Model_v44.xlsx. Four years of company financials, three years of forecasts. Line items stacked thirty-deep. Cost of goods sold. Sales and marketing. Personnel. Operations. Host and infrastructure. Other operating expenses. D&A. The model was built by an intern two months into his analyst rotation. The intern was smart. The intern went to Oxford. But the intern had made a fundamental error in his cost forecasting.

She scrolls through the detail tabs. There—in "Cost Structure Assumptions." Support costs: marked as "Fixed at £187k." She writes in her notebook: "Support scales with headcount. Headcount scales with customer count. If you double customers, you roughly double support tickets. You need more support staff. Cost is not fixed."

She scrolls down to the summary. The intern's model shows: Revenue growing from £5.3m to £16.6m by FY28. EBITDA negative in year one (which is correct, Subtrax is currently unprofitable). EBITDA turns positive in year two—£4.2m, climbing to £9.8m by year three. Revenue trajectory unbroken, smooth curve upward. Too smooth. Too neat.

She knows what happens when you treat support as fixed: when you add customers, costs don't magically stay flat. Support tickets rise. Support tickets need responses. Responses need people. People need desks, laptops, Slack subscriptions, training. The cost rises with headcount. Not perfectly (you don't add one person per customer), but directionally. You can't keep support costs flat while your customer base triples.

Same with infrastructure. The intern marked it as "variable per invoice volume" which is correct, but the percentage was too low. The intern assumed hosting costs grow at 8% of invoice volume. Trace checks the cost of AWS. For a company with 5,000 daily invoices processing, the monthly bill is roughly £12,000. For 10,000 daily invoices (double volume), the bill is not £24,000. It's more like £19,000 because of AWS's scaling discounts and their bulk commitment. But it's still significantly more. The intern underestimated the step-function cost structure.

She clicks into the assumptions and rewires the mechanics. Support costs as a percentage of revenue—5% instead of fixed. Host costs scaled more realistically. She adds a tab for "Semi-variable costs" where you can model step functions (every time you hit X customers, you need a new support person). She recalculates. The summary recompiles.

The new trajectory is radically different. Revenue still grows to £16.6m. But EBITDA: negative in year one (same). Negative in year two—now -£1.1m instead of +£4.2m. The inflection point is delayed. EBITDA finally turns positive in year three at just £0.8m. Then climbs, but more slowly. By year four: £3.2m, not £9.8m.

She stares at the new numbers. Same revenue growth. Same trajectory. But the path to profitability is slower, deeper, requires longer patience. And patience is what matters. Because a model that shows profitability in year two is a different investment than one that shows it in year three.

She makes a note in clean handwriting: "Numbers fixed. Cost structure corrected. EBITDA trajectory more realistic per actual cost scaling. Intern made smart assumptions but missed semi-variable cost structure. Clean version on the drive. — TF"

She saves the file and emails it to herself: "Subtrax_Model_v45_CORRECTED." She closes the lid. The screen goes black. She pulls her jacket from the back of the chair and walks to the lift. Descends. The 17th floor disappears. The lobby appears. The night air hits her face. She walks to the DLR. The carriage is mostly empty—a cleaning crew, two night-shift workers, a taxi driver getting home from an airport run. She gets in and sits. Yellow streetlamps blur past the Thames. She leans back and lets the motion rock her. She thinks about the model. Tomorrow someone will take her corrected version and build a pitchbook on it. That pitchbook will form the basis of a valuation. That valuation will determine whether Subtrax gets funded or not.

She doesn't know this. But her three-hour model correction is now doing real work in the world.

Tomorrow, at 06:47 AM, Le Pitch will read her email. At 07:00, he will walk into the office and see her name on the summary. At 07:15, he will call her. "Brilliant work on Subtrax," he will say. "See me 7:30, before the German call. Something new." She will say yes, but she won't understand why until she walks into his office and he says: "There is a manufacturer in Germany. Interesting situation. Margins look stable but something feels off. I need an analyst on the ground. One month, Würzburg." She will pause for half a second. She will think about her brother's exams. She will think about her parents. And then she will say yes. Because this is how careers are made—one corrected model at a time. One decision at a time. One month that becomes a year.

She doesn't know yet that this moment—23:52 on a Tuesday night, alone at a desk in Canary Wharf—is the pivot point. That the month in Würzburg will become the hinge upon which this entire series turns. That this corrected model, and this decision, will weave her life into the story of two companies and three other characters she hasn't met.

She just knows she's tired and the numbers look right now and the DLR is warm and the city outside is blurring past and tomorrow belongs to the bank but tonight belongs to this moment of completion.

Investment banking looks intimidating. Not because the ideas are hard. Not because the maths is complex. It's intimidating because the explanations usually are.

Every finance textbook writes as if you should already understand the concepts. They start with: "Valuation is the process of determining the economic value of a business." That tells you nothing. It's like explaining an orchestra by saying "many instruments playing together." Technically true. Fundamentally useless.

This series exists to fix that.

Before we start, let's be clear about what this is not. This is not a training program for investment bankers. Investment bankers spend 5 years learning how to serve the interests of senior partners while appearing to serve clients. This is not that.

This is not a get-rich-quick scheme. Finance is not a shortcut to wealth. It is a framework for thinking. Wealth comes from creating value. Finance is just the language for describing it.

This is not a MBA program. You will not learn Greek letters (sigma, beta, lambda) or obscure theorems (CAPM, efficient markets hypothesis, Black-Scholes). Those are tools. Important tools, but not where we start.

This is not boring. If it becomes boring, you're reading it wrong. Every concept in corporate finance is illustrated here by real people making real decisions with real consequences. Financial concepts without human context are just math. Add humans and they become stories.

Every Goldman once began as a greenhorn. The difference between them and you is not talent. It is exposure.

Five characters. Two companies. One story. No formulas to memorise. By the end, you will understand corporate finance well enough to walk into any meeting and hold your own.

Think of this series as a novel about numbers. Each chapter opens with a scene—a conflict, a problem, a decision point. The conflict illustrates a financial concept. By the end of the chapter, through the story of real people making real choices, you will understand not just the concept but why it matters.

Read this on your own time. Fifteen to twenty minutes per chapter. Don't rush. There are no quiz questions, no homework, no grade. Just read. Let the characters live. Watch them make decisions. Understand why they make them. Notice how the decisions are all connected even though the characters don't know it.

If you get lost in the numbers, re-read the scene. The scene always explains the number. If you get lost in the concept, skip ahead to the next scene. The next scene will show you how it plays out in the real world.

If you find yourself thinking: "This is exactly what happens in my company," pause and write it down. Those moments are where the learning happens. Finance is most useful when you can apply it to something you actually care about.

And if at any point you feel confused, remember this: confusion is information. It means the concept hasn't quite landed. That's okay. Let it sit. In Chapter 2, in Chapter 5, in Chapter 8, it will land. Finance is not about instant understanding. It's about cumulative understanding. The numbers don't make sense in isolation. They only make sense when you see how they connect.

A quick note on the title: "From Greenhorn to Gold(wo)man."

A greenhorn is a newcomer, an amateur, someone who doesn't know the rules of the game yet. In finance, the term used to be literal. Young people came from families with money, or families without money, and got access to the game through universities or connections. The game was investment banking: advising clients on M&A, valuations, IPOs. The game was closed to outsiders. You either came from the right background or you didn't.

In 2026, the game has opened. You don't need a pedigree to learn finance. You just need curiosity and time. You can learn from the internet. You can build models. You can read deals. You can understand how companies work. The barriers to entry are now conceptual, not institutional.

A Goldman Sachs banker—whether they're actually at Goldman or somewhere else—is someone who has learned to think about business in a specific way. Not necessarily right or wrong, but consistent. They think in multiples. They think in leveraged returns. They think in deal structures. They think about value creation and value capture. When they look at a business, they don't see a company. They see a set of levers and a target return.

But the first Goldman you ever meet was once a greenhorn too. They didn't understand the concepts on day one. They learned. They were exposed to deals. They read financial statements. They asked questions. They got yelled at for getting it wrong. They got praised for getting it right. Over time, the concepts became intuitive. The way of thinking became automatic.

That is what this series does. It accelerates that learning. Instead of five years of investment banking, you get eleven chapters and understanding of the core concepts.

The title says: "The difference between them and you is not talent. It is exposure."

This deserves unpacking. It is absolutely true, and it is also misleading.

It is true that most people in finance are not specially talented. They are people who were exposed to finance early (through family, school, luck) and who learned by doing. They read the first balance sheet and didn't understand it. They read the hundredth and started to see patterns. By the time they read the thousandth, they could glance at it and extract meaning instantly. That is not genius. That is exposure.

But it's also misleading, because exposure alone is not enough. You can be exposed to finance for five years and still not understand it if you're not paying attention. The person who reads balance sheets passively, without asking questions, without connecting to actual businesses, will not learn.

That is why this series pairs concept with story. Exposure + narrative understanding = real learning.

You can read about EBITDA in a finance textbook and forget it in a week. But if you see Trace fixing a model where the intern got the cost structure wrong, and you understand why that matters, and you see how that decision cascades into a larger deal—you will remember it. You will have context. You will have intuition.

This series is designed to give you the thousand-balance-sheet equivalent in eleven chapters. Not by teaching you formulas or definitions, but by exposing you to real problems, real decisions, real people making real choices.

That is the promise. By the end, you won't be a Goldman. But you will think like one. You will have the exposure. The difference between you and them will no longer be talent or opportunity. It will be years of practice. And that you can build over time, now that you have the foundation.

One thing before we move on: this series will use finance terminology. Terms like "EBITDA," "working capital," "debt/EBITDA," "CAC," "LTV." But we'll always explain them in the context of the story. The terms are not the point. Understanding what they describe—what actually happens in a business—is the point.

If you find yourself forgetting a term, that's okay. Go back and find where we explained it in context. That context matters more than the term.

Vocabulary
Lingo You'll See in This Series

EBITDA: Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization. Essentially: profit, before you pay debt and taxes. It's the cash that the business generates from operations.

Working Capital: Money trapped in the business cycle. Customers owe you money (receivables). You have inventory. You owe suppliers. The net of these determines how much cash you need just to operate.

Debt/EBITDA: A measure of leverage. If a company has €60m debt and €10m EBITDA, it's 6x levered. That's risky. 3-4x is standard for industrial businesses. 2x or less for risky businesses.

CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost): How much it costs to acquire one customer. If you spend £1.8m in sales and marketing and acquire 100 customers, CAC is £18k.

LTV (Lifetime Value): How much revenue you make from one customer over their life. If the customer pays £19.5k per year and stays 3 years, LTV is £58.5k. You want LTV >> CAC or you're not building a business.

Multiple (EV/EBITDA): How much buyers are willing to pay for a euro of EBITDA. If Königshof is worth €80m and generates €10m EBITDA, it trades at 8x. If Subtrax is worth £30m and (once profitable) will generate £5m EBITDA, it's valued at 6x. Multiples vary by industry, growth rate, and risk.

Margin: Profit as a percentage of revenue. Gross margin (profit before operating expenses). Net margin (profit after everything). Higher is better, but context matters.

DCF (Discounted Cash Flow): A valuation method. Projects five years of cash flows and discounts them back to today to calculate value. Based on the idea that a business is worth the cash it will generate in the future.

LBO (Leveraged Buyout): Buying a company using mostly debt. You put in equity, borrow the rest, improve operations, and sell at a profit. The debt amplifies returns if the business improves.

Churn: The percentage of customers that leave each period. Low churn is good. High churn means you're leaking revenue. For subscription businesses, churn is the single most important metric.

We'll introduce more terms as we go. When you see a new term, look for the context. The story explains it better than the definition ever could.

A brief explanation of what you'll actually learn in each chapter, beyond the titles.

Chapter 2 (Profitability): Wilhelm and Schilling argue about whether to cut prices to fill capacity. The answer hinges on understanding operating leverage—how sensitive profit is to volume. Trace fixes a model where the intern got the cost structure wrong. Eddie discovers that a company can be profitable and still cash-negative. By the end, you will understand the income statement not as a financial document but as a story about how revenue flows through an organization into profit. And more importantly, you will understand why the same revenue line can mean completely different things depending on what's underneath it.

Chapter 3 (Solvency): Magda discovers that £1.3m of Subtrax's cash is frozen by a payment processor. On paper, Subtrax is profitable. In reality, it can't make payroll. The chapter walks through balance sheets, working capital, and cash flow statements. By the end, you will understand why "profit" and "cash" are not the same thing. You will understand why a company can be technically profitable but financially insolvent.

Chapter 4 (Patterns): Haircut looks at two companies with the same revenue and asks: are these the same business? One is industrial (Königshof). One is SaaS (Subtrax). They have completely different economics. The chapter walks through business model archetypes and unit economics. By the end, you will understand that the income statement is just the beginning. What matters is the shape of the business underneath it.

Chapter 5 (Projections): Trace sits late into the night building financial models. She learns that modeling is not about predicting the future. It's about stress-testing your assumptions. By the end, you will understand how to build a model, how to run scenarios, and more importantly, how to know when your model is probably wrong.

Chapter 6 (LBOs): Haircut decides to buy Königshof using leverage. The deal structure: Skarn puts in equity, banks lend debt, combined leverage grows the business and increases returns. By the end, you will understand why private equity exists and how the math actually works.

Chapter 7 (Financing): Wilhelm receives the offer and asks: why would I ever borrow money if I have €40m in cash? Le Pitch explains. Capital structure is not a random decision. It's a choice about optimal leverage. By the end, you will understand how to think about capital structure and why even rich companies borrow.

Chapter 8 (Valuation I): Trace has to value Königshof. She uses three methods: trading comps, precedent transactions, and DCF. All three give different answers. By the end, you will understand the "football field" of valuation and why it's never one number.

Chapter 9 (Valuation II): Haircut tears apart Trace's valuation. He questions every assumption. He asks about working capital adjustments, earnout structures, quality of earnings. By the end, you will understand that valuation is not science. It's judgment. It's where the art of finance lives.

Chapter 10 (M&A I): The deal process begins. Buyers are identified. Management presentations happen. Bids are received. By the end, you will understand the mechanics of how deals actually happen and why strategic buyers sometimes win.

Chapter 11 (M&A II): Signing day. Wilhelm signs the papers. Now the accounting begins. Purchase price allocation, goodwill, deferred tax. By the end, you will understand that a deal doesn't end when the parties shake hands. It ends when it hits the books.

That's the journey. Eleven chapters. Five characters. Two companies. One story.

These five characters—Le Pitch, Trace, Haircut, Wilhelm, and Magda—operate in a system. They do not know each other yet. But their decisions will weave together.

Le Pitch decides which deals SBCI pursues. Trace executes the deals. Haircut decides which deals Skarn pursues. Wilhelm decides whether to sell. Magda decides whether to take a call.

None of them are in a room together. None of them are coordinating. But each decision cascades into the others. Le Pitch's decision to pursue Königshof depends on Haircut's conviction about the deal. Haircut's conviction depends on Trace's ability to understand Wilhelm. Wilhelm's decision depends on what Trace tells him. Magda's decision to take the call depends on how tired she is that day.

This is how the world actually works. Not with grand conspiracies or brilliant plans. But with five people making independent decisions that happen to create a pattern. The pattern creates a deal. The deal changes their lives.

A word about how this series works. Each chapter is structured like a television episode. It opens with a scene—a moment, a problem, a conflict. It explores a single concept through that conflict. It closes with another scene, usually the same characters at night, processing what happened.

The chapters are sequential but they are also independent. If you read only Chapter 2 (Profitability), you will understand profit vs cost vs margin. If you read only Chapter 6 (LBOs), you will understand leverage. But if you read them in order, you will understand something else: how five people and two companies become entangled in a deal that changes all of them.

Pay attention to the financial numbers. They always mean something. When Wilhelm has €40m in cash and zero debt, that is not a random fact. When Trace fixes a model that understates costs, that is not busywork. When Haircut sees a 15-18% IRR, he is seeing not a return but a story. Every number contains a story.

The chapters progress from conceptual to practical. Chapters 1-4 are about how to think. Chapters 5-8 are about how to build. Chapters 9-11 are about how to close. By Chapter 11, the same characters who met in Chapter 1 will have made decisions that cascade through everything. You will understand why.

After eleven chapters, you will be able to:

Understand financial statements. Not just read them, but understand what they mean. Why a profitable company can run out of cash. Why a loss-making company can still be valuable. Why two businesses with the same revenue can have completely different futures.

Think like an investor. Not about making money (though that matters). But about the levers that move value. Revenue growth. Cost efficiency. Capital efficiency. Leverage. Timing.

Read a deal. When you hear "we're acquiring X for Y," you will understand what "acquiring" means. What Y is justified by. What risks are embedded in that price. Whether the deal is brilliant or a disaster waiting to happen.

Articulate intuition. You will be able to walk into a room and say things like: "The problem is not revenue, it's unit economics. The CAC is reasonable but the LTV is too thin. They need to either cut marketing spend or increase ASP." And the people in the room will nod. Because you are not reciting buzzwords. You are thinking like them.

That is the real goal of this series. Not to teach you finance. But to teach you how to think like a financier.

The Real Difference
Why Königshof and Subtrax Matter to This Series

Königshof is the textbook industrial business. Asset-heavy. Capital-intensive. Low growth, high cash generation (if volume stays stable). Cyclical. Dependent on macro factors (interest rates, commodity prices). Vulnerable to technology disruption. Family-owned, which brings both stability (long-term thinking) and risk (succession planning).

In Chapter 2, you'll see how Königshof's profitability looks stable but is actually sensitive to volume. A 20% drop in units cuts EBITDA by far more than 20%. That's operating leverage. In Chapter 3, you'll see how Königshof's €40m cash buffer is both its strength and a warning sign—it means the founder doesn't think his business has bright future, so he's building a fortress. In Chapter 4, you'll see how Königshof's margins look average until you understand the unit economics. Then they look solid. In Chapter 6, you'll see how a PE fund would buy Königshof using leverage—borrowing against the cash flow to finance an LBO. In Chapter 11, you'll see what happens when that leverage meets a downturn.

Subtrax is the textbook SaaS business. Asset-light. Capital-efficient. High growth, low near-term profitability. Non-cyclical. Dependent on product-market fit and unit economics. Venture-backed or founder-led, which brings both optionality (many paths forward) and chaos (decisions get made on the fly). Recurring revenue creates a powerful flywheel: happy customers renew, generating cash to invest in acquiring new customers.

In Chapter 2, you'll see how Subtrax's gross margins (71%) dwarf Königshof's (32%), but Subtrax is loss-making because it spends heavily on acquisition and headcount. That's optionality. In Chapter 3, you'll see how Subtrax's negative working capital model (customers pay upfront, you pay bills monthly) is beautiful—free cash generation once you're profitable. In Chapter 4, you'll see how Subtrax's growth rate masks the fact that until it reaches scale, it's essentially reinvesting its gross profit into more acquisition. In Chapter 5, you'll see how sensitivity to churn is Subtrax's biggest risk—a 2% swing in churn swings valuation by 30%. In Chapter 8, you'll see how Subtrax's valuation uses DCF more than comps, because public comparables are rare. In Chapter 10, you'll see how Subtrax would be valued for acquisition.

Together, they illustrate a fundamental truth: There is no single right way to build a business. Königshof's conservatism has kept it independent for 120 years. Subtrax's aggressiveness is making it valuable after 5. One maximizes for longevity. The other maximizes for scale. Both are reasonable. Both have downsides.

By the end of this series, you will understand not just how to value them, but why someone would want to buy them. And that is the beginning of real financial intuition.

Trace teaches you how to build financial models that actually mean something. How to take assumptions and turn them into forecasts. How to understand when costs scale and when they don't. How to catch mistakes that other people miss. How to work late into the night on something that matters. Her strength is diligence. Her weakness is that she sometimes mistakes hard work for good work.

Haircut teaches you how to protect yourself. How to ask tough questions even when everyone else is excited. How to model the downside case. How to understand what can go wrong and price for it. How to see through narratives to the numbers underneath. His strength is skepticism. His weakness is that he can't see upside when it's actually there.

Wilhelm teaches you how to build something that lasts. How to prioritize long-term durability over short-term profits. How to keep your people loyal by being loyal to them. How to say no to most things so you can do a few things exceptionally well. How tradition and craft create competitive advantages that spreadsheets can't measure. His strength is clarity of purpose. His weakness is that the world changes faster than he does.

Magda teaches you how to build something people actually want. How to listen to customers instead of consultants. How to ship fast and iterate based on feedback. How to care more about the product than the valuation. How to build a culture that attracts the right people. Her strength is product instinct. Her weakness is that she doesn't always understand the business she's built.

Here's something important that most finance education gets wrong: corporate finance is not abstract. It is not removed from real life. It is real life, but viewed through a different lens.

When you understand that Le Pitch is trying to create a narrative that will convince Wilhelm to sell, you're not learning about "deal origination." You're learning how incentives shape behavior. When you understand that Trace is fixing a cost model because she sees a flaw that nobody else sees, you're not learning about "financial modeling." You're learning what diligence actually means. When you understand that Haircut is questioning whether the numbers can be trusted, you're not learning about "due diligence." You're learning about the burden of making decisions with incomplete information.

Finance is not different from the rest of human experience. It just adds numbers to it.

Before we close, a word about the five characters you've just met.

Le Pitch represents the deal side. The person who initiates, who creates demand, who frames the narrative. He is not evil. He is not necessarily greedy. He is just someone who sees the world through the lens of the deal. Everything is a transaction. Every problem is an "opportunity." Every conversation is a negotiation. This mindset creates deals. It also creates blind spots.

Trace represents the execution side. The person who has to make the narrative true. She is smart, but not brilliant. She is hardworking, but not obsessive. She is the kind of person who fixes problems that nobody asked her to fix, because she sees them and can't unsee them. This mindset creates good work. It also creates burnout.

Haircut represents the investor side. The person who keeps score. Who questions the narrative. Who asks: but what if volume declines? But what if churn worsens? But what if the founder leaves? This mindset protects capital. It also creates paralysis.

Wilhelm represents the founder side. The person with something to lose. He has built something that has lasted 120 years. He has kept it independent. He has kept it honest. He has succeeded by saying no to most opportunities and yes to only the best ones. This mindset creates durability. It also creates vulnerability to people who are better at saying yes.

Magda represents the creator side. The person who built something from an idea. She doesn't think about transactions or returns or deals. She thinks about whether the product solves a real problem. She thinks about whether her team is happy. She thinks about whether the code is elegant. This mindset creates beautiful businesses. It also makes them fragile when they scale.

These five characters are not stereotypes. They are archetypes. Every deal, every company, every moment of corporate history has someone playing each role. Sometimes it's one person playing multiple roles (the founder who also has to execute, the banker who also has to invest). But the roles are always there. Understanding these roles is the first step to understanding how finance actually works.

This is what corporate finance teaches you, if you pay attention: that the world is a series of competing perspectives, each reasonable, each with downsides, and that deals exist to navigate between them. Not to eliminate conflict, but to manage it. To create value by acknowledging that what you have (€40m in cash, a family business, 120 years of history) is worth more to someone else (who has operating expertise, growth capital, a path to scale) than it is to you.

That's the real lesson. Finance is the language for managing tradeoffs. It's the framework for understanding what happens when different incentives collide.
